Title: George Washington (Athenaeum Portrait)

Artist: Gilbert Stuart

Year: 1796

Style: Neoclassicism

This painting was never finished, yet it became the most famous portrait in American history — the image of Washington on every U.S. dollar bill originates from this "unfinished" work.

In 1796, Washington's wife Martha commissioned portrait painter Gilbert Stuart to create a second portrait of her husband. Stuart meticulously rendered Washington's face — the resolute gaze, the tightly pressed lips, the commanding expression — but then he deliberately set down his brush. The background shows only a faint undercoat, the collar and shoulders are merely outlined, and the entire painting looks like an exquisite head floating on an empty canvas.

This was not laziness, nor a lack of inspiration. It was a shrewd business strategy. Stuart understood the allure of the "unfinished": it is mysterious, it is unique, it makes people forever imagine "what it would look like if completed." More importantly, as long as the original remained unfinished, he could keep it in his possession and use it as a master copy to continuously produce and sell replicas. He was proven right — Stuart made over 70 copies from this unfinished portrait, selling each for $100, a considerable sum at the time.

Stuart never delivered the original to Martha Washington, and when he died in 1828, the painting was still in his studio. It was eventually acquired by the Boston Athenaeum library, hence its name "Athenaeum Portrait." Ironically, it was precisely this "unfinished" state that elevated it above every completed portrait of Washington, making it the definitive image of America's founding father.

The unfinished background strips away the constraints of era and setting, transforming Washington's face into an eternal symbol. When the U.S. Treasury chose the design for the dollar bill in 1869, they selected not some grand, complete historical painting, but this "unfinished" portrait of just a face. From that moment on, everyone who uses a dollar bill gazes upon this deliberately preserved blank — a perfection created through the art of incompletion.
