Title: Alexander and Bucephalus

Artist: Edgar Degas

Year: 1859–1862 (never completed)

Style: Impressionism / History Painting

Degas's "Alexander and Bucephalus" is a painting about taming — but what was tamed was not the wild horse in the picture, but the artist himself. This painting sat in Degas's studio for over fifty years, enduring countless revisions, reversals, and restarts, until his death in 1917, when it remained in its "unfinished" state.

The scene depicts an ancient legend: the 12-year-old Alexander the Great taming Bucephalus, a horse no one else could control. The young Alexander stands on the left side of the canvas in a white robe, with the restless horse behind him and astonished onlookers all around. But strangely, the painting looks like a collage of two different works: the horse is meticulously rendered, every muscle clearly defined, while the foreground features bold, rough brushstrokes, with some figures' arms and legs seemingly "vanishing" into the canvas, as though they were never painted at all.

This was not a matter of skill, but a symptom of perfectionism. Degas was never satisfied with this painting. He revised, repainted, and adjusted it over and over, but each change only pushed him further from "perfection." The horse was too finely detailed, making the figures look crude; if he refined the figures, the overall balance collapsed. He was trapped in an endless cycle — as he himself once said: "I tend to start a hundred things and never finish one."

Degas's perfectionism was legendary. Clients who purchased his paintings learned a trick: as soon as a work arrived, they would immediately lock it to the wall or secure it with chains, because Degas would sneak into their homes, take the painting down, and continue reworking it. His friends joked that Degas's paintings were never "finished" — they merely "escaped" from his studio.

In 1917, Degas passed away at the age of 83. A vast trove of unfinished works was discovered in his studio — some had been set aside for decades. "Alexander and Bucephalus" was among them, the exquisitely rendered horse still gazing forward while the rest of the canvas remained forever in a state of drafting. Ironically, it is precisely this "incompleteness" that makes the painting so moving: it is not a story of an ancient hero taming a horse, but a story of an artist who spent his entire life unable to tame the perfectionism within his own heart.
